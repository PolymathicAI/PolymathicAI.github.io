Biological function emerges from coupled constraints spanning sequence, structure, regulation, evolution, and cellular context, yet most foundation models in biology are trained within a single modality or for a fixed forward prediction task.

# Jointly Modeling Biomolecular State

  The central dogma of molecular biology, which describes the flow of genetic information from DNA to RNA to protein, is often visualized as a linear, deterministic pipeline. However, biological reality is far more intricate: it is a probabilistic web of feedback loops where downstream structures constrain upstream sequences, and environmental contexts dictate regulatory outcomes.

### The LORE Dataset

  To enable MIMIC's flexibility, we first introduce LORE, a curated dataset that aligns heterogeneous biological measurements—sequence, structure, conservation, regulatory assays, surface chemistry, and semantic annotations, into coherent, partially observed molecular examples anchored to shared transcripts and proteins. Because LORE provides non-redundant, cross-modal supervision rather than isolated single-modality corpora, MIMIC can learn the complementary constraints between modalities that drive its strongest performance gains: auxiliary context helps most precisely where it provides information that sequence alone leaves ambiguous.
</div>

### The MIMIC Framework

<div style="overflow: auto;">
  <video width="400" style="float: right; margin: 0 0 16px 20px;" controls>
    <source src="/images/blog/mimic/AnyToAnyFlow.mp4" type="video/mp4">
  </video>

  MIMIC is designed as an all-to-all generative model over biomolecular modalities rather than a collection of task-specific predictors. Its split-track encoder-decoder architecture groups together signals that share a natural coordinate system (such as nucleotide-resolved features along DNA or RNA and residue-resolved features along proteins) while preserving semantically distinct inputs like function, taxonomy, and experimental context as separate token groups. This allows the model to fuse aligned measurements where biology demands it, without collapsing fundamentally different entities into a single stream. Trained with a masked multimodal modeling objective, MIMIC can take in arbitrary subsets of observed modalities and reconstruct or generate the missing ones, enabling the same model to support completion, prediction, and design across the central dogma.
</div>

MIMIC is a fully open source model. The MIMIC model weights, code, and the LORE dataset will be released publicly in the coming months.

## MIMIC Learns Powerful Representations for Downstream Inference

MIMIC’s encoder representations transfer strongly across a broad range of downstream tasks. On mRNABench, which covers RNA function, localization, and translation regulation, MIMIC matches or outperforms Evo 2 and Orthrus on most tasks. It shows especially strong gains on GO function prediction and protein localization, where aligned protein modalities likely offer a direct advantage. The most notable improvement appears on MRL-Sugimoto, a benchmark for mean ribosome load across endogenous human transcript isoforms, suggesting that multimodal pretraining captures features tied to isoform-level translational output that sequence-only models miss. On a comprehensive protein benchmark spanning structure, function, interaction, and developability, MIMIC ranks first or near first on most evaluations, including antibiotic resistance, BindingDB, enzyme classification, and solubility. It also outperforms specialized protein language models such as ESM-2, ESM3, PGLM, SaProt, and ProTrek, many of which are substantially larger. Together, these results suggest that joint multimodal pretraining yields richer representations than protein sequence alone, even with far fewer parameters.

## MIMIC Enables Iterative, Conditional Biomolecular Design

#### MIMIC for RNA Design - Aberrant Splicing Mutations

A central promise of biological generative models is the ability to redesign sequence context to restore a desired regulatory outcome. We examined this in the setting of the well-characterized HBB IVS-II-654 C>T mutation, a deep intronic variant that disrupts normal splicing by activating cryptic splice sites and promoting pseudoexon inclusion. We conditioned the model on the desired wild-type splice pattern while keeping the causal mutation fixed, and asked it to redesign nearby sequence context within editable windows of varying size and position. The resulting designs substantially reduced the predicted likelihood of aberrant splicing, with PhyloP conditioning further improving design quality. Importantly, successful corrections could be found even when the editable region did not directly overlap the cryptic splice sites, suggesting that multimodal conditioning enables the model to discover indirect, yet biologically plausible ways of restoring regulatory function.

#### MIMIC for Protein Design - Structured Binders

Protein design poses an analogous challenge, but in a different regime: viable sequences must satisfy not only the geometric constraints of a target fold, but also the surface properties that govern molecular interactions. Because MIMIC jointly represents sequence, structure, and surface features, it can condition directly on both backbone geometry and the chemical environment of an interface. We tested this in design settings centered on two therapeutically relevant targets, PD-L1 and ACE2, using several conditioning strategies based on backbone information, surface features, or their combination. Across both systems, design quality improved as additional modalities were provided, with joint conditioning on backbone and full surface information producing the largest fraction of high-confidence sequences. These designs were not only predicted to recover the desired structural features, but also remained diverse in sequence space, indicating that the model was exploring multiple viable solutions rather than reproducing a single template. Taken together, these results suggest that multimodal conditioning can substantially narrow the protein design search space, steering generation toward sequences that are both structurally compatible and consistent with the surface properties required for binding.

### What comes next

MIMIC covers a meaningful subset of the central dogma, but important layers such as chromatin state, nucleic-acid chemical modifications, translational regulation, and downstream cellular phenotypes remain absent or underrepresented. Broadening modality coverage, extending context scale, and experimentally validating designed sequences are natural next steps. In its current form however, MIMIC demonstrates that joint modeling over aligned molecular state captures cross-modal dependencies that no single-modality model can access, with practical value already evident across prediction, representation learning, and generative design.
